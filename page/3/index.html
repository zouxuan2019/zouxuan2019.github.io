How to Blur the background in Portraits

  • Use a large aperture, say, in the range of f/2 to f/4.
  • Increase the distance between your subject and the background.
  • Decrease the distance between you and the subject.
  • Use a longer focal length lens.

Try Panning

  • Select a shutter speed that’s slightly slower than you’d normally use - perhaps by one or two stops. For example, if you’d normally shoot at 1/125 seconds, lower it to 1/30 seconds.
  • As the subject moves in front of the camera, move the camera along with it, maintaining the subject in the frame. As you do, keep your finger on the shutter button, pressing it down halfway to lock the focus.
  • Press the shutter all the way down, ensuring that you continue the panning motion, even after the shutter has fired.
  • More details watch videos from here https://youtu.be/lnpc6QsoKo4

Sunny 16 Rule

  • On a sunny day, set your aperture to f/16, and then set the shutter speed and ISO values to the inverse of one another. So, if the ISO is set to 200, the shutter speed would be 1/200 seconds. If the ISO is set to 100, the shutter speed would be 1/100 seconds.
  • F16(Sunny)->F11(Cloudy)->F8(Overcast)->F5.6(Heavy Overcast)->F4(Sunset)->F2.8->F2->F1.4->F1 The F setting doubles the light than the one in front.
  • If you keep ISO constant, say 100, in order to get same exposure,increase aperture from F16 to F11 will lead to a double faster shutter speed, namely from 1/100 to 1/200
  • More details refer to :https://www.photographytalk.com/beginner-photography-tips/understanding-the-sunny-16-rule

Keeping one of the settings on Auto in most light varying environment

In most non-studio environments, we do not have control over the lighting conditions. It can be anything from passing clouds to walking through a museum where each room is lit differently.
Running all 3 settings on manual may leave you scrambling to balance your exposure.
Keeping one of the settings on Auto is a quick workaround.

  • In order to achieve 1 Auto, you may refer to the following guidelines
    • Auto Aperture: “S” Mode + Maunal ISO
    • Auto Speed: “A” Mode + Manual ISO
    • Auto ISO: “M” Mode + Auto ISO
  • The last one is my personal favourite,it helps rejecting unintended blur images caused by a slow shutter speed. That leaves ISO to do the heavy lifting.

Shade

  • Shade can be your best friend.
  • If there is no way you can make the available light work for your photo, shoot in the shade.
  • You’ll get a nice even exposure with no patchy highlights throughout your shot.

Limit your palette

  • When photos have too many colours spewing out from them, they’re often hard to look at.
  • Unless it’s a photo of a rainbow or the Mardi Gras.
  • Try to focus on having one or two colours predominately featuring in your photograph.
  • It will be more pleasing to the eye and will help set the tone of the image.

Photography Tips - 3

Share

Shape with light

Never shoot with the sun directly behind you. It creates boring, flat light on the subject. If you shoot with the light source to the side or behind the subject, you are able to shape with the light, creating a more interesting photo. — Patria Jannides

Focal length

Keep it simple. I shoot with two prime lenses and one camera; A 28mm and a 35mm. For everything. I use the 35mm lens 70% and the 28mm lens 30% of time. It takes some time to get used to it, but once you work it out, shooting primes is the only way to go. It means you have to work with what you have and you can’t be lazy. Basically, this means more pictures and less fiddling around with zooming and maybe missing moments. It also helps for consistency. If you’re working on a project or a series, keeping the same focal lengths is a great way to maintain a powerful sense of consistency. — Justin Wilkes

Shoot with your mind

Even when you’re not shooting, shoot with your mind. Practice noticing expressions and light conditions. Work out how you’d compose a picture of that scene over there that interests you, and what sort of exposure you might use to capture it best. — Leah Robertson

Photography Tips - 2

Share

Utilize the pohtography “golden hour”

  • The golden hours is about a one-hour window briefly after sunrise or before sunset.
  • The longer shadows and especially the more diffused light during these periods provide much more flattering light.
  • You’re much less likely to ‘blow out’ lightlights or lose detail in the shadows that are difficult to avoid during the strong light available during most of the day.

Get a low cost reflector to drastically increase your options

  • Having a reflector will let you better control light on your subject.
  • Foldable fabric ones are also available at photography stores.
  • The black side lets you block or reduce lights, while the white side can be used to fill in shadows.
  • If there is too much contrast in your scene, use a reflector to fill the shadows on your subject.

Portrait photography tips

  • Use window light if you don’t have a studio or fancy lighting equipment.
  • Turn off all the lights in the room and move near a window with some curtains so you can play around with diffusing the light.
  • Turning off all the lights include the pop-up flash on your camera too.
  • Make sure you focus on the eyes, make your subjects feel comfortable.

Photography Tips - 1

Share

Manual Focus

  • Shooting in manual focus mode is a skill that you need to learn and practice.
  • Manual focus is more reliable to produce more precise photos.

Angles - Mix it up

  • Don’t just shoot from eye level! This can get boring so mix up your angles to keep the viewer engaged and tell your story in an interesting and unique way.
  • Get high, get low, look up and down and all around to find the perfect shot!

Enjoy your photography

  • Experiment with what you can capture
  • Photograph new subjects
  • Take your time
  • Keep learning and improving
  • Share your photos
  • Explore new ideas and interact with other photographer
  • Build yourself a portfolio
  • Don’t be too hard on yourself! Photographs is fun, so have fun with it!
